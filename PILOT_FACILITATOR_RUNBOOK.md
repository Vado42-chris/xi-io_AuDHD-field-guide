# Pilot Facilitator Runbook

## Why this runbook exists

This runbook is for the person guiding or observing a trusted pilot session.

Its purpose is to keep sessions:
- consistent
- calm
- comparable
- focused on product learning

It should be used alongside:
- the participant brief
- the pre-pilot regression checklist
- the pilot observation session template

---

# 1. Facilitator posture

The facilitator is not there to defend the product.
The facilitator is there to learn from the participant.

That means:
- do not over-explain too early
- do not rescue the product too quickly
- do not lead the participant toward “correct” answers
- do not argue with confusion
- do not turn a pilot into a demo

The session should reveal what the product currently does well and where it still breaks down.

---

# 2. Before the session

Confirm the following before starting:
- the intended build is loaded
- the relevant regression gate has been reviewed
- the participant has the pilot brief
- observation template is ready
- any caveats you need to state are ready in plain language

If something is already known to be unstable, do not hide that.
State it clearly.

---

# 3. Opening script

Suggested opening:

> Thanks for helping with this pilot. This product is still being refined, so today is about learning where it is clear, where it is confusing, and where it feels useful or not useful. This is not a test of you. If something feels unclear, that is helpful information for the product. Please be direct.

Optional follow-up:

> You can pause, skip, or stop at any point if needed. The goal is careful product learning, not pressure.

---

# 4. What not to say too early

Avoid opening with things like:
- “It works like this...”
- “What you’re supposed to do is...”
- “Most people understand this part...”
- “That section means...”

Only clarify after you have observed whether the product stood on its own.

---

# 5. Recommended session flow

## Step 1
Let the participant orient themselves briefly.

Prompt:
- “Take a look at this screen and tell me what you think it is for.”

Watch for:
- first-read comprehension
- hesitation
- where their eyes go first
- what they think the main action is

---

## Step 2
Observe the main Help Now path.

Prompt:
- “What would you do first here?”

Watch for:
- whether they understand current state selection
- whether they can identify starter supports
- whether more-options/deeper-detail controls feel obvious or distracting

---

## Step 3
Observe outcome logging and reflection.

Prompt:
- “If you had tried that, what would you do next?”

Watch for:
- whether outcome logging is understandable
- whether optional reflection feels truly optional
- whether the flow feels coherent

---

## Step 4
Observe Learn Me comprehension.

Prompt:
- “What do you think this screen is trying to tell you?”

Watch for:
- whether they understand the top interpretation layer
- whether uncertainty guidance makes sense
- whether the screen feels worth returning to

---

## Step 5
Observe personalization reactions.

Prompt:
- “Do any of these personalized parts feel meaningful to you?”

Watch for:
- custom state labels
- pinned comforts
- current-state-aware prioritization
- support view preference memory

---

# 6. Useful neutral prompts

Use prompts like:
- “What do you think this means?”
- “What would you click next?”
- “What do you expect to happen?”
- “How certain or cautious does this feel?”
- “What part feels most helpful?”
- “What part feels least clear?”
- “Would you come back to this?”

These prompts are better than explanatory prompts.

---

# 7. When to clarify

Clarify only after one of these is true:
- the participant has already shown genuine confusion
- the session would stall completely without clarification
- the participant explicitly asks for explanation

When clarifying, keep it brief.
Then return to observation.

---

# 8. What to capture carefully

Capture especially:
- confusion around the first next step
- trust-language reactions
- places where the product feels too certain
- places where the product feels too vague
- whether Learn Me feels personally useful
- whether personalization feels meaningful or decorative
- whether progressive disclosure helps or hides too much

---

# 9. Red-flag observations

Flag immediately if the participant:
- misunderstands the product as a medical authority
- feels pressured by the tone
- cannot identify the main support loop
- interprets early patterns as guaranteed truth
- is confused enough that the product feels unreliable

These are higher-priority issues than simple cosmetic preferences.

---

# 10. Stop conditions during session

Pause or stop the session if:
- the participant becomes too uncomfortable
- the product behaves in a clearly broken way that invalidates the session
- the session turns into facilitator-led explanation rather than observation
- a known blocker makes further observation misleading

If you stop, note exactly why.

---

# 11. End-of-session questions

Suggested closing questions:
- “What felt most useful?”
- “What felt least clear?”
- “Did the trust language feel right, too strong, or too vague?”
- “Would you come back to this?”
- “What is the first thing you would change?”

---

# 12. After the session

Immediately after the session:
- fill the observation template while memory is fresh
- separate direct observation from interpretation
- record any strong quotes or paraphrases
- note whether anything discovered is a pilot blocker

Do not wait too long.
Small details disappear quickly.

---

# 13. Facilitator reminder

A good pilot session is not one where the product looks perfect.
A good pilot session is one that reveals the truth clearly enough to improve the product.

That is the standard.
